The Only Emperor is the Emperor of Ice Cream: Presentation, Branding and Identity

By Jeron Paul January 18, 2025 6 min read

What separates the forgettable from the legendary? Sometimes, it's not the power of the idea itself—it's how it's presented.

"What is the difference between a villain and a super-villain? PRESENTATION!" – Megamind

Megamind's line is funny because it's true. The difference between "just another player" and a breakout startup often comes down to branding, presentation, and identity—the story you tell and how you tell it.

Wallace Stevens captured something similar in his strange, haunting poem The Emperor of Ice Cream. On the surface, the poem is about mortality. But tucked inside its imagery is an insight about the role of surface-level appeal. The emperor is not emperor of iron or stone—he is emperor of ice cream. Of indulgence. Of fleeting pleasure. He rules because he commands attention in the present.

Entrepreneurs can learn something here. Ice cream is thrilling, immediate, even hedonistic. Great branding works the same way—it delights, excites, and makes people stop scrolling. At its best, branding isn't just sugar on top of substance. It's what makes people notice you in the first place.

But Stevens goes deeper:

"Let be be finale of seem.
The only emperor is the emperor of ice cream."

What seems—what appears on the surface—is not to be dismissed. It is the finale of being. In business, your presentation and brand don't just sell your product—they actively shape the identity of your customers. Branding tells your customer who they are when they choose you.

A productivity tool isn't just an app—it's a declaration: I am efficient.

A design platform isn't just software—it says: I am creative.

A financial automation system isn't just code—it signals: I am trustworthy and in control.

The emperor of ice cream is powerful not because ice cream lasts, but because it commands desire and identity in the moment. In the same way, your brand and presentation aren't window dressing—they're the emperor itself.

So, the lesson for founders is Megamind-simple: the difference might not be the tech or the features. It might just be—presentation.
